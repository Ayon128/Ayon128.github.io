Representation Learning: A Gentle Introduction

Md. Ayon Mia · April 2026 · 5 min read
"The goal of representation learning is to find a transformation of the data that makes it easier to extract useful information for a given task."

What is a Representation?

When we work with raw data — a sentence, an image, a user's click history — computers can't directly reason over the surface form. They need numbers. But not just any numbers: the representation should capture the structure and semantics of the input in a way that is useful for downstream tasks.

Think of the word king. You could represent it as its index in a vocabulary (say, 5421). That number tells the model nothing about its meaning. But if you represent it as a high-dimensional vector where king - man + woman ≈ queen, suddenly the geometry of the space is encoding semantic relationships.

That is the magic of representation learning — finding vector spaces where proximity reflects meaning.

From One-Hot to Embeddings

The earliest NLP representations were one-hot vectors: a sparse binary vector with a 1 at the word's index and 0 everywhere else. These are orthogonal by construction — every word is equally distant from every other word. There is no way to encode that cat and dog are more similar than cat and airplane.

Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) changed this. By training a shallow neural network to predict surrounding words (CBOW) or to use a word to predict its context (Skip-gram), it learned dense low-dimensional vectors where semantically similar words cluster together. These static embeddings had a major limitation: a single fixed vector per word regardless of context. The word bank has the same vector whether you're talking about a river bank or a financial bank.

Contextual Representations: The Transformer Era

ELMo (2018) was among the first to produce context-sensitive representations using bidirectional LSTMs. Then came the shift that changed everything: the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) with its self-attention mechanism.

BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) used Transformers pre-trained on masked language modelling (MLM) and next-sentence prediction (NSP). Crucially, BERT produces a different representation for each occurrence of a word depending on its full context. The model learned to encode rich syntactic and semantic structure by attending over all other tokens simultaneously.

  • BERT: Bidirectional encoder, strong at understanding tasks (classification, NER, QA)
  • GPT family: Autoregressive decoder, strong at generation tasks
  • XLM-RoBERTa: Multilingual BERT variant, handles 100+ languages — central to my own research on low-resource NLP

Why Does This Matter for Low-Resource NLP?

For languages like Bangla, Tamil, or Malayalam — where labelled data is scarce — the quality of representations is everything. Pre-trained multilingual models like XLM-RoBERTa encode cross-lingual knowledge acquired from large multilingual corpora. By fine-tuning on a small labelled dataset in a low-resource language, we transfer the rich representations learned from higher-resource languages.

Much of my research explores how to extract, combine, and adapt these representations for tasks like sentiment analysis, misogyny detection, and fake news classification in Bangla and Dravidian languages. Feature fusion strategies — pooling CLS tokens, mean-pooling token embeddings, CNN over token sequences — are different ways of summarising what the model has learned into a single fixed-size vector useful for classification.

Key Takeaways

  • A representation is a mapping from raw input to a vector space designed to preserve useful structure.
  • Learning good representations often makes downstream tasks dramatically easier.
  • Pre-training on large corpora and fine-tuning on task-specific data is the dominant paradigm in NLP today.
  • For low-resource languages, leveraging multilingual pre-trained models is the most effective strategy available.
